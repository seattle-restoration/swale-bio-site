Proprietary Formulation

The Swale Mix.

Three Pacific Northwest natives, engineered to work together. Planted in naturalistic drifts — seasonal structure that performs year-round.

Salal

Gaultheria shallon

Evergreen groundcover native to the PNW. Dense root system stabilizes soil and prevents erosion. Leathery leaves shed water into the swale channel.

Western Sword Fern

Polystichum munitum

Massive fronds intercept rainfall, slowing velocity before it reaches the swale floor. Root networks create natural biofilter channels in the soil media.

Slough Sedge

Carex obnupta

The workhorse of wetland filtration. Dense clumping growth traps sediment. Root zone hosts microbial communities that break down petroleum compounds and heavy metals.

How we plant.

Three methods. One philosophy: bring in the emotion of nature.

Primary Method

Matrix Planting

Interwoven species at ground level — a living tapestry where each plant supports its neighbors. No gaps, no mulch, no maintenance. The bioswale fills itself in.

Accent Method

Naturalistic Drifts

Large sweeps of single species — Piet Oudolf's signature. Creates movement and seasonal rhythm across the swale's contours.

Structural Method

Block Planting

Bold masses of a single species for maximum visual impact. Defines zones, creates edges, and anchors the composition at key transitions.

Your bioswale pays for itself.

King County charges a Surface Water Management fee based on your property's impervious surface percentage. More pavement and roof = higher fee. A bioswale converts impervious area into permeable, living infrastructure — dropping you into a lower rate class and saving real money every year.

Very Heavy (85-100%) $5,495/acre/yr
Heavy (65-85%) $4,466/acre/yr
Mod. Heavy (45-65%) $3,206/acre/yr
Moderate (20-45%) $1,879/acre/yr

Source: King County DNRP — 2025 SWM Fee Rates
